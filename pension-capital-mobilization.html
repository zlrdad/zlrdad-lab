Venture capital dramatically outperforms buyouts — the asset class European pensions already invest in heavily. The barrier to pension VC allocation isn't returns. It's regulation, risk perception, and inertia. Pension funds know the returns justify the allocation. They're held back by legal constraints and outdated mental models about what constitutes a "prudent" investment.

Canada's Position

The Scale Problem
VCCI 4.0 at C$1B is the smallest peer program by a large margin. Germany commits 18× more. The UK commits 90× more.
World-Class Managers, Misaligned Capital
Canadian pension funds (CPP, OTPP, CDPQ, OMERS) are among the world's most sophisticated institutional investors — but they deploy billions into US and global VC, not Canadian startups.
The Paradox
Canada has world-class pension fund managers investing billions in US venture capital while Canadian startups struggle to raise Series A rounds and compete for capital against US companies.

This isn't a talent problem. Canada has exceptional founders, strong research institutions, and deep technical expertise across sectors from software to biotech. It's a capital allocation problem. The ecosystem lacks sufficient early-stage capital at the scale required to compete globally.

What's Needed

1
Scale Up

VCCI needs to expand 5–10× to match peer programs. C$5–10B would position Canada alongside France and Germany.

2
Match Structure

Adopt Israel's 0.3:1 co-investment model. Government capital as leverage attracts private capital without distorting markets or creating dependency.

3
Domestic Allocation Incentive

Encourage (don't mandate) Canadian pension funds to allocate a meaningful share to Canadian VC. Tax incentives or priority access to co-investment could align capital with domestic opportunity.
